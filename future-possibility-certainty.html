Future Possibility and Certainty

Expressing Possibility: may, might, could

Use may, might, or could to say that something is possible in the future — you are not sure if it will happen. All three are followed by the base verb.

I might still be at home with my parents next year. (possible, not sure)
You could live with a friend. (possible option)
You may find something cheap outside the city. (possible)
She might not come to the party. (possibly won't happen)

Expressing Near Certainty: probably

Use probably when you are almost sure something will or won't happen. Place probably after will in positive sentences, and before won't in negative sentences.

I will probably get a new job next year. (almost certain — positive)
I probably won't have enough money for that. (almost certain — negative)
She will probably be late.

Expressing Full Certainty: definitely

Use definitely when you are completely sure something will happen. Place definitely after will in positive sentences, and before won't in negative sentences.

I will definitely miss them! (100% certain — positive)
He definitely won't forget your birthday. (100% certain — negative)
💡 Certainty scale:
could / may / might → possible (maybe 30–50%)
will probably → almost certain (around 70–80%)
will definitely → completely certain (100%)

⚠ Common mistakes:
✗ She might to come tomorrow. → ✓ She might come tomorrow. (no to after modals)
✗ I definitely will go. → ✓ I will definitely go. (definitely comes after will)
✗ I probably won't to pass. → ✓ I probably won't pass.
