Future Time
Clauses

I'll call you when I arrive. (not "will arrive")

when / before / after present simple
The golden rule

No "will" in the time clause

A time conjunction (after, before, when…) connects two parts of a future sentence. The time clause always uses present simple — never will.

Examples

I will call you when I arrive.

Finish your homework before you watch TV.

The structure

main clause (will/going to) + conjunction + present simple

Or: conjunction + present simple + , + main clause

The conjunctions

Five time conjunctions

when
at the moment that
I will call you when I arrive.
before
earlier than
Finish your homework before you watch TV.
after
later than
After she graduates, she is going to travel.
as soon as
immediately when
I'll text you as soon as I get the results.
until
up to the point that
He won't stop until he gets it right.
💡 Word order

Either clause can come first.

I'll text you as soon as I get the results. (no comma)

As soon as I get the results, I'll text you. (comma needed)

In real sentences

See it in action

We will leave when everyone is ready.
Before he starts the job, he will need to sign a contract.
She is going to rest until she feels better.
⚠ Watch out

Common mistakes

I will call you when I will arrive.

I will call you when I arrive.

She won't leave before she will finish.

She won't leave before she finishes.

As soon as he will get home, he'll eat.

As soon as he gets home, he'll eat.

Until she will arrive, we'll wait here.

Until she arrives, we'll wait here.

Recap

Remember this

Time clause
present simple, always
Main clause
will / going to
Conjunctions
when, before, after, as soon as, until
Order
comma only if time clause is first

Never "will" in the time clause — even though it's about the future.
