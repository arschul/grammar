past & present 6 tenses
The big picture

Six tenses — one map

Simple Past
verb + -ed / irregular
Completed action, often with yesterday, ago
Past Progressive
was/were + -ing
In progress at a specific past moment
Past Perfect
had + past participle
Earlier of two past events
Past Perfect Progressive
had been + -ing
In progress before a past moment
Present Perfect
have/has + past participle
Past event connected to or relevant now
Present Perfect Progressive
have/has been + -ing
Started in the past, still continuing now
Past tenses

Simple Past vs Past Progressive

Simple Past — completed
She visited her grandmother last Sunday.
✓ Finished event. Uses time marker: last Sunday.
They didn't finish the project on time.
✓ Completed in the past — clearly over.
Past Progressive — in progress
He was reading when the phone rang.
✓ In progress → interrupted by simple past.
What was happening inside their bodies when they were migrating?
✓ Two actions both in progress at the same past moment.
Present tenses

Present Perfect vs Progressive

Present Perfect — result / experience
No butterfly has ever made the whole trip.
✓ Experience up to now; still relevant.
She has worked here for five years.
✓ Period from past to now; connection to present.
Pres. Perfect Progressive — still continuing
The number has been falling since the 1980s.
✓ Started in the past, still happening now.
I have been studying English for three years.
✓ Ongoing action — not finished.
Further back in time

Past Perfect vs Past Perfect Progressive

Past Perfect — earlier completed event
Scientists hadn't found an explanation.
✓ They hadn't found it — before they eventually did.
By the time we arrived, the show had already started.
✓ Show started first → we arrived second.
Past Perf. Progressive — duration before a moment
Scientists had been asking questions for many years.
✓ Ongoing up to a past point — emphasises duration.
She had been waiting for an hour when the bus came.
✓ Action in progress right up to a past moment.
⚠ Watch out

Common mistakes

I have seen him yesterday.

I saw him yesterday. → finished time = simple past

She had been working when I called.

She was working when I called. → in progress at that moment = past progressive

They have been lived here for ten years.

They have been living here. → need -ing after have been

By the time I arrived, she already left.

She had already left. → earlier past event = past perfect

Recap

Remember this

Simple Past
Completed — with finished time (yesterday, ago)
Past Progressive
In progress at a past moment — interrupted or simultaneous
Present Perfect
Past event relevant now — experience, result, or news
Perfect Progressive
Ongoing action from past to now (or to a past point) — emphasises duration

Ask yourself: when did it happen, is it finished, and does it still matter?
