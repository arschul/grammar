Adjective Order

What Is Adjective Order?

When you use more than one adjective before a noun, English follows a fixed sequence. The categories from first to last are:

[Quantity] [Opinion] [Size] [Age] [Shape] [Colour] [Origin] [Material] + Noun

four big old square wooden houses
a pretty little blue French electric car
a small, square white rock

Opinion Always Comes First

Opinion adjectives (beautiful, horrible, amazing, lovely) always come before fact adjectives (size, colour, origin, material). This is the most important rule to remember.

a beautiful old painting → opinion, then age
a long thin silver necklace → size, then shape, then colour
a big red Italian sports car → size, then colour, then origin

Size → Shape → Colour → Origin → Material

For fact adjectives, follow the sequence strictly. You won't always use all categories — two or three adjectives is the most common in natural speech.

a small square wooden box → size → shape → material
a huge old brick building → size → age → material
a long dark wooden corridor → size → colour → material
a stunning old French painting → opinion → age → origin

Commas: When to Use Them

Use a comma between two adjectives of the same general group (e.g. two opinion adjectives). Do not use a comma between adjectives from different categories.

a friendly, helpful teacher ✓ → two opinions: use a comma
a small red bag ✓ → size and colour: no comma
an amazing, big pink salt lake ✓ → opinion before size/colour: comma after opinion only
⚠ Common mistakes:
✗ a red big car → ✓ a big red car (size before colour)
✗ a wooden old French table → ✓ an old French wooden table (age → origin → material)
✗ a pretty, Italian, small vase → ✓ a pretty small Italian vase (opinion → size → origin, no comma between different categories)
