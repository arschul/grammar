Degree & Intensifiers

So / Such

What are So and Such?

So and such are both used to intensify meaning, often followed by a result clause with that. The key difference lies in what follows them.

SO + adjective / adverb
SUCH (A/AN) + (adjective) + noun

Using SO

So goes before an adjective or adverb (without a noun).

The film was so boring that I fell asleep. (adjective)
She speaks so quickly that I can't understand her. (adverb)
He ran so fast that he broke the record. (adverb)

Using SUCH

Such goes before a noun phrase (adjective + noun, or noun alone).

It was such a beautiful day that we went to the park. (adj + singular countable noun)
They are such kind people that everyone loves them. (adj + plural noun)
It was such bad weather that we stayed inside. (adj + uncountable noun)
He told such funny jokes that we all laughed. (adj + plural noun)
💡 Quick test: Can you replace the word with very? If yes → use so. Does it go before a noun? → use such.

⚠ Common mistakes:
✗ It was so a good film. → ✓ It was such a good film.
✗ She is such beautiful. → ✓ She is so beautiful.

Without a Result Clause

The that clause is often omitted in spoken English.

The food was so delicious!
It was such a long journey.
