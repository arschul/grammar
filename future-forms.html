Future Forms

Four Ways to Talk About the Future

English has four main ways to express the future. Each one is used in a different situation.

will — predictions, spontaneous decisions, offers
going to — intentions and plans, predictions based on evidence
present progressive — fixed arrangements with another person
simple present — scheduled events (timetables, programmes)

Will

Use will for general predictions about the future, and for spontaneous decisions made at the moment of speaking.

I think it will rain tomorrow. (prediction)
She won't make that mistake again. (negative prediction)
Will you try harder next time? (question)
I'll have the pasta, please. (spontaneous decision)

Going To

Use going to for intentions and plans already decided before the moment of speaking, and for predictions based on something you can see or know right now.

I'm going to study law. (intention — already decided)
It's not going to rain. (prediction based on evidence)
Are you going to travel? (question about plans)

Present Progressive

Use the present progressive for fixed arrangements — things you have organised with another person at a specific time.

You're having lunch with her tomorrow. (arrangement)
He's seeing her tomorrow. (fixed plan with someone)
Is he visiting her tomorrow? (question about arrangement)

Simple Present for the Future

Use the simple present for events that are part of a fixed schedule or timetable — things that happen at set times regardless of personal choice.

School starts at 9 a.m. (timetable)
We leave at 3 a.m. (scheduled departure)
The train doesn't leave until tomorrow morning.
⚠ Common mistakes:
✗ I going to study tonight. → ✓ I'm going to study tonight. (need am/is/are)
✗ The film wills start at 8. → ✓ The film will start at 8. (will never takes -s)
✗ I will to call you later. → ✓ I will call you later. (will + base verb, no to)
